Overview

To respond effectively to disease outbreak situations, policymakers need to be able to anticipate the geographic and temporal movements of potential epidemics, as well as predict the effects of various intervention strategies. This model attempts to address both of these needs.

The Descriptive Model

To accurately portray the spread of the epidemic across time and space, the model combines three layers: A representation of the U.S. broken up into 200 “metapopulations”, each centered around a major airport; an epidemiological compartment model describing the disease behavior within each metapopulation; and a transportation network describing flows between metapopulations.

The Decision Model

The descriptive model was used to answer the specific question: How should the U.S. government respond to a major smallpox attack in New York City? There were four main objectives: (1) Minimize mortality, (2) End the epidemic ASAP, (3) Minimize cost, (4) Minimize vaccine-related deaths.

Various combinations of the following four interventions were tested against the above objectives: (1) Quarantine + targetted/ring vaccination, (2) Full vaccination only in metapopulations with identified infecteds, (3) Full vaccination across the country, (4) Travel restrictions.

Highlighted Results

  • Quarantine + ring vaccination is the most effective strategy when quarantine efficiency exceeds a threshhold of around 80%. Below this threshhold, however, the strategy prolongs the epidemic while failing to prevent significant casualties.
  • Countrywide vaccination brings the quickest end to the epidemic, shows the lowest overall mortality and variation in mortality, but involves the highest number of vaccine-related deaths.
  • Longer latency periods and relatively low prodromal infectiousness favor quarantine while inverse conditions favor vaccination.

Highlighted Spatial-Temporal Results

Travel restrictions have very little impact on mortality, but do alter the spacial dynamics of the epidemic, concentrating deaths in relatively few areas. This may aid quarantine efforts (though this wasn’t modelled).

A Major Weakness

This model suffers from a number of weaknesses, the most important being that quarantine efficiency is assumed, not modelled.
